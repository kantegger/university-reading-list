### Part Two: The Institutions of Government

#### Composition Of Parliament

#### Role and Functions of Parliament

This section explains Parliament's central legislative, scrutiny, and representative functions. It details how Parliament enacts laws, debates national issues, and authorizes government expenditure. The section highlights Parliament's role in holding the executive to account through questioning ministers, committee investigations, and debates. It also covers the representational function, whereby Members of Parliament (MPs) advocate for their constituents and contribute to shaping public policy.

#### Privileges and Powers of Parliament

This section outlines the unique legal privileges enjoyed by Parliament, such as freedom of speech in parliamentary proceedings (ensured by Article 9 of the Bill of Rights 1689), the principle of exclusive cognisance (Parliament's right to regulate its own affairs), and the power to summon witnesses and request documents. These privileges are essential for enabling Parliament to perform its legislative and scrutiny functions without external interference, thereby supporting the integrity and independence of the legislative process.

#### The Crown and Royal Prerogative

This section describes the constitutional role of the Crown, which is now largely ceremonial, with real political authority exercised by elected ministers. It examines the royal prerogative—residual powers historically vested in the monarch, such as the issuing of passports, the conduct of foreign affairs, and the granting of honours. The section discusses how, in modern times, these powers are exercised by government ministers and are subject to legal and constitutional limitations, including judicial review.

#### Cabinet, Government Departments, and Civil Service

#### Public Bodies and Public Appointments

This section summarizes the different types of public bodies in the UK, including non-departmental public bodies (NDPBs), executive agencies, and advisory committees. It explains how these bodies carry out specific governmental functions at arm's length from ministers. The section also describes the process and importance of public appointments, which ensure appropriate oversight, expertise, and accountability in the governance of these bodies.

## Key Concepts:

### Parliamentary Supremacy

**Historical Evolution:** Parliamentary supremacy means that Parliament is the supreme legal authority in the UK, capable of creating or ending any law. The historical evolution of this principle highlights the gradual shift of power from the monarchy to Parliament.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Despite challenges from EU membership and the Human Rights Act, parliamentary supremacy remains a cornerstone of the UK constitution. However, Brexit and devolution have introduced new complexities and potential limitations.

### The Rule of Law

**Principles:** The rule of law emphasizes that all individuals and institutions are subject to and accountable under the law. It includes principles of legal certainty, equality before the law, and fair access to justice.

**Applications:** The rule of law is applied in various contexts, such as the limitation of government powers, protection of fundamental rights, and ensuring fair judicial processes.

### Responsible and Accountable Government

**Collective Responsibility:** This principle means that all government ministers are collectively responsible for government policies and decisions. If the government is defeated in a vote of no confidence, all ministers must resign.

**Individual Ministerial Responsibility:** Ministers are accountable to Parliament for their personal conduct and the actions of their departments. This ensures a mechanism for scrutinizing and holding ministers accountable for their decisions.

### Human Rights Act 1998

**Incorporation of ECHR:** The Human Rights Act incorporates the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law, allowing individuals to seek redress in domestic courts for human rights violations.

**Key Provisions:** The Act includes rights such as the right to life, prohibition of torture, right to a fair trial, and right to privacy. It has significantly influenced the development of UK human rights jurisprudence.

### Judicial Review

**Grounds of Review:** Judicial review allows courts to review the lawfulness of decisions or actions taken by public authorities. Grounds for review include illegality, procedural unfairness, and irrationality.

**Procedures and Remedies:** The judicial review process includes specific procedures and potential remedies, such as quashing orders, prohibiting orders, and declarations of unlawfulness.

### Devolution

**Structure and Powers:** Devolution refers to the delegation of powers from the central government to regional governments in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. Each devolved government has varying degrees of legislative power.

**Impact on Constitutional Structure:** Devolution has reshaped the constitutional structure of the UK, creating a more complex and multi-layered governance system. It raises questions about the distribution of powers and the balance between regional autonomy and central control.

### The Crown and Royal Prerogative

**Prerogative Powers:** The Crown and Royal Prerogative refers to the powers exercised by the monarch on the advice of the government. These powers include the ability to dissolve Parliament, appoint ministers, and grant royal assent to legislation.

**Limitations:** The Crown and Royal Prerogative is subject to constitutional and legal limitations, including the doctrine of parliamentary sovereignty and the Human Rights Act.

### Freedom of Expression and Association

**Legal Protections:** The right to freedom of expression is protected by common law, statutes, and international agreements like the ECHR. This includes the right to speak, write, and publish freely, subject to limitations for reasons such as national security and public order.

**Public Order and Regulation:** Freedom of association encompasses the right to form and join groups, including political parties and trade unions. Regulations may restrict these freedoms to ensure public order and safety.

### Administrative Law

**Functions and Scope:** Administrative law governs the activities of administrative agencies of government. It ensures that such bodies act within their legal powers and adhere to principles of fairness, reasonableness, and transparency.

**Tribunals and Inquiries:** Administrative law includes mechanisms like tribunals and public inquiries to resolve disputes and investigate issues within the administrative framework.

### State Security and Official Secrets

**Legal Framework:** State security laws regulate the activities of intelligence agencies and the protection of official secrets. These laws aim to balance national security needs with individual rights and freedoms.

**Oversight and Accountability:** There are mechanisms to oversee and ensure the accountability of security agencies, such as parliamentary committees and independent regulators.

### Special and Emergency Powers

**Legislative Measures:** Special and emergency powers include laws that grant the government extraordinary powers during crises, such as the Civil Contingencies Act 2004. These powers can affect civil liberties and require careful oversight.

**Implications for Civil Liberties:** The use of emergency powers raises concerns about the potential for abuse and the need for safeguards to protect individual rights during emergencies.

### Judicial Independence

**Principles and Protections:** Judicial independence ensures that judges can make decisions free from political pressure or influence. It is essential for maintaining the rule of law and public confidence in the legal system.

**Challenges and Reforms:** The chapter discusses ongoing challenges to judicial independence and recent reforms aimed at strengthening the judiciary's autonomy and integrity.

## Critical Analysis:

### Parliamentary Supremacy and Brexit:

**Strengths:** The principle of parliamentary supremacy has been fundamental in establishing the UK as a sovereign state with a strong, centralized legislative authority. It allows Parliament to respond flexibly to changing political and social conditions without being constrained by entrenched legal provisions.

**Critique:** Brexit has exposed vulnerabilities in the principle of parliamentary supremacy. The referendum result and subsequent parliamentary maneuvers demonstrated the complexities and tensions in a system where parliamentary sovereignty can clash with direct democratic mandates. The challenge lies in reconciling the supremacy of Parliament with the will of the people as expressed through referendums.

### The Rule of Law and Counter-Terrorism:

**Strengths:** The rule of law ensures that all actions by public authorities are legally grounded and that individuals have access to justice. This principle is crucial in maintaining public confidence in the legal system and protecting individual rights.

**Critique:** Counter-terrorism measures often test the boundaries of the rule of law. Laws like the Terrorism Act 2000 and the Investigatory Powers Act 2016 grant extensive powers to security agencies, which can undermine civil liberties. The balance between national security and individual rights remains a contentious issue, with ongoing debates about the appropriate scope and oversight of such powers.

### Responsible Government and Ministerial Accountability:

**Strengths:** The doctrines of collective and individual ministerial responsibility ensure that government actions are subject to scrutiny and that ministers are accountable for their conduct and decisions. This fosters transparency and accountability in government.

**Critique:** In practice, ministerial accountability can be weakened by political considerations and party loyalties. Instances where ministers have avoided resignation despite significant failures highlight the limitations of these doctrines. The effectiveness of accountability mechanisms depends on political will and the robustness of parliamentary oversight.

### Human Rights Act 1998:

**Strengths:** The incorporation of the ECHR into UK law through the Human Rights Act 1998 has strengthened the protection of individual rights. It allows individuals to seek redress in domestic courts and has influenced the development of a human rights culture within the UK.

**Critique:** The Human Rights Act faces political challenges, with some advocating for its repeal or replacement with a British Bill of Rights. Critics argue that the Act sometimes conflicts with parliamentary sovereignty and can lead to judicial overreach. Balancing the protection of rights with respect for democratic decision-making is an ongoing challenge.

### Judicial Review:

**Strengths:** Judicial review serves as a crucial check on the exercise of public power, ensuring that decisions and actions by public authorities comply with the law. It upholds principles of legality, rationality, and procedural fairness.

**Critique:** The expansion of judicial review has led to concerns about judicial activism, where courts are perceived to be encroaching on the domain of the executive and legislature. This tension raises questions about the appropriate limits of judicial intervention in administrative and political matters.

### Devolution and Constitutional Complexity:

**Strengths:** Devolution has allowed for greater regional autonomy and responsiveness to local needs in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. It reflects the diverse political and cultural landscape of the UK.

**Critique:** Devolution has introduced constitutional complexities and potential conflicts between devolved and central governments. Issues such as the Scottish independence movement and differing approaches to Brexit highlight the challenges of maintaining a cohesive constitutional framework while accommodating regional diversity.

### The Crown and Royal Prerogative:

**Strengths:** The royal prerogative provides the executive with flexibility to act in certain areas without the need for parliamentary approval, which can be useful in times of emergency or for conducting foreign affairs.

**Critique:** The use of prerogative powers without adequate parliamentary oversight can undermine democratic accountability. Recent judicial decisions, such as the Miller cases, have emphasized the need for greater scrutiny and limitation of these powers to ensure they are exercised in a manner consistent with constitutional principles.

### Freedom of Expression and Association:

**Strengths:** Legal protections for freedom of expression and association are essential for a vibrant democracy. They enable individuals to participate in public discourse, criticize the government, and organize collectively.

**Critique:** These freedoms can be restricted for reasons such as national security, public order, and protection from harm. Determining the appropriate balance between protecting these rights and imposing necessary limitations is challenging. Controversies over hate speech laws and restrictions on protests illustrate the difficulties in navigating this balance.

### Administrative Law and Public Accountability:

**Strengths:** Administrative law provides mechanisms for holding public authorities accountable and ensuring they act within their legal powers. It includes processes like judicial review, tribunals, and public inquiries.

**Critique:** The complexity of administrative law can make it difficult for individuals to navigate and access justice. There are also concerns about the effectiveness of remedies and the resources available to support administrative justice. Ensuring that administrative law remains accessible and effective is an ongoing concern.

### State Security and Civil Liberties:

**Strengths:** Laws governing state security and the protection of official secrets are necessary for safeguarding national security and protecting sensitive information. They provide a legal framework for the operations of intelligence agencies.

**Critique:** The potential for abuse of state security powers poses significant risks to civil liberties. Oversight mechanisms are essential to prevent misuse of these powers and to ensure that actions taken in the name of national security are proportionate and justified.

### Special and Emergency Powers:

**Strengths:** Special and emergency powers enable the government to respond swiftly and effectively to crises, such as natural disasters, terrorist attacks, and pandemics. These powers are crucial for maintaining public safety and order.

**Critique:** The use of emergency powers can lead to the suspension of civil liberties and democratic processes. Ensuring that these powers are subject to rigorous oversight and are used only when absolutely necessary is critical to preventing abuse and protecting democratic values.

### Judicial Independence:

**Strengths:** Judicial independence is fundamental to the rule of law and ensures that judges can make decisions free from political pressure or influence. It is vital for maintaining public confidence in the legal system.

**Critique:** Judicial independence can be threatened by political pressures, public criticism, and insufficient safeguards. Ensuring that the judiciary remains independent and adequately protected from external influences is essential for the integrity of the legal system.

## Real-World Applications and Examples:

### Parliamentary Supremacy and Brexit:

**Example:** The Brexit process has vividly illustrated parliamentary supremacy and its limits. In the case of R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union (2017), the UK Supreme Court ruled that the government could not trigger Article 50 of the Lisbon Treaty without an Act of Parliament. This decision reinforced the principle that significant constitutional changes require parliamentary approval.

**Application:** This case underscored the role of Parliament in overseeing significant national decisions, highlighting the need for parliamentary consent in the Brexit process. It also demonstrated the judiciary’s role in upholding parliamentary sovereignty against executive overreach.

### The Rule of Law in Counter-Terrorism:

**Example:** The Investigatory Powers Act 2016 (often referred to as the "Snooper's Charter") expanded the surveillance capabilities of UK intelligence agencies, raising concerns about the balance between security and privacy. The Act allows for the bulk collection of communications data and internet records.

**Application:** This legislation has been challenged in courts, with critics arguing that it violates the rule of law by granting excessive powers to the state without adequate safeguards. Ongoing legal challenges and public debate highlight the tension between national security and individual rights.

### Ministerial Accountability and Government Transparency:

**Example:** The Windrush scandal, which came to light in 2018, involved the wrongful detention and deportation of Commonwealth citizens who had the right to live in the UK. The scandal led to the resignation of Home Secretary Amber Rudd after it was revealed that she had misled Parliament about deportation targets.

**Application:** This incident highlighted the importance of ministerial accountability and transparency. It demonstrated the mechanisms of parliamentary scrutiny and the consequences for ministers who fail to uphold their responsibilities.

### Human Rights Act 1998:

**Example:** In A and Others v Secretary of State for the Home Department (2004), the House of Lords (now the Supreme Court) ruled that the indefinite detention of foreign terror suspects without trial, as provided by the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001, was incompatible with the Human Rights Act 1998. Specifically, it violated Article 5 (right to liberty and security) and Article 14 (prohibition of discrimination) of the ECHR.

**Application:** This case demonstrated the power of the Human Rights Act to protect individual rights against disproportionate state action. It also showed the judiciary’s role in interpreting and enforcing human rights protections within domestic law.

### Judicial Review and Executive Actions:

**Example:** In R (UNISON) v Lord Chancellor (2017), the Supreme Court ruled that the government's introduction of employment tribunal fees was unlawful because it effectively denied access to justice. The fees were found to be prohibitive for many individuals, undermining the right to a fair trial.

**Application:** This case illustrates the crucial role of judicial review in safeguarding fundamental rights and ensuring that executive actions are lawful and reasonable. It reinforces the judiciary's ability to check executive power and protect access to justice.

### Devolution and Regional Autonomy:

**Example:** The Scottish Parliament's handling of education and healthcare policies demonstrates the impact of devolution. For instance, Scotland has implemented different policies on tuition fees and prescription charges compared to the rest of the UK.

**Application:** Devolution allows for tailored policy-making that reflects regional preferences and needs. It highlights the benefits of regional autonomy within a unified state, while also raising questions about the coherence and consistency of national policies.

### Royal Prerogative and Judicial Oversight:

**Example:** The case of R (Miller) v Prime Minister (Miller 2) (2019) involved the Prime Minister’s advice to the Queen to prorogue Parliament for five weeks during the Brexit negotiations. The Supreme Court ruled that the prorogation was unlawful because it had the effect of frustrating or preventing the ability of Parliament to carry out its constitutional functions without reasonable justification.

**Application:** This case demonstrated the judiciary's willingness to scrutinize and limit the exercise of prerogative powers. It affirmed the principle that executive actions, even those involving prerogative powers, must be subject to legal and constitutional constraints.

### Freedom of Expression in the Digital Age:

**Example:** The case of PJS v News Group Newspapers Ltd (2016) involved a privacy injunction to prevent the publication of details about a celebrity’s private life. The Supreme Court upheld the injunction, balancing the right to privacy against freedom of expression.

**Application:** This case illustrates the ongoing challenges in balancing freedom of expression with the right to privacy, especially in the context of digital media. It underscores the importance of legal frameworks in protecting individuals’ rights while allowing for a free press.

### Administrative Law and Public Accountability:

**Example:** The Grenfell Tower Inquiry, established to investigate the causes and consequences of the Grenfell Tower fire in 2017, highlights the role of public inquiries in administrative law. The inquiry aims to ensure accountability and prevent future tragedies.

**Application:** Public inquiries serve as a vital tool for administrative accountability, allowing for thorough investigation of public failures and providing recommendations for reform. They play a critical role in maintaining public trust and ensuring that government actions are scrutinized.

### State Security and Oversight Mechanisms:

**Example:** The Intelligence and Security Committee (ISC) of Parliament oversees the operations of the UK’s intelligence agencies, including MI5, MI6, and GCHQ. The ISC reports to Parliament on issues of national security, ensuring that intelligence activities are conducted lawfully.

**Application:** Oversight mechanisms like the ISC are essential for balancing state security needs with individual rights. They provide a check on the activities of intelligence agencies, promoting transparency and accountability.

### Special and Emergency Powers in Health Crises:

**Example:** The COVID-19 pandemic saw the UK government invoking emergency powers under the Public Health (Control of Disease) Act 1984 and the Coronavirus Act 2020 to impose lockdowns, quarantine measures, and other restrictions to control the spread of the virus.

**Application:** The use of emergency powers during the pandemic highlighted the need for robust legal frameworks and oversight to ensure that such powers are used appropriately and do not disproportionately infringe on civil liberties. It also demonstrated the importance of clear communication and public trust in the effectiveness of emergency measures.

### Judicial Independence and Public Confidence:

**Example:** The appointment and tenure of judges in the UK are designed to ensure judicial independence. For instance, the Judicial Appointments Commission (JAC) selects judges based on merit, and judges enjoy security of tenure, meaning they cannot be removed from office without substantial cause.

**Application:** These safeguards help maintain public confidence in the judiciary by ensuring that judges are impartial and free from political influence. Judicial independence is crucial for upholding the rule of law and ensuring fair administration of justice.
